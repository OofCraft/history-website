Life and Accomplishments

It is a well-known fact that Harriet Tubman helped free a lot of slaves in her period. She worked for the Underground Railroad and directed slaves to British Canada where they could be free. As famous as she was, it is less known about her continued fight for African Americans and women’s rights after the war. Even after helping thousands of slaves to freedom, she continued to improve the quality of life and access to necessities of her people for years after. She was an important figure in American history. Harriet Tubman was an important figure in American history who represents the freedom of African Americans. Harriet Tubman was born into slavery in 1820 on a plantation located in Dorchester County, Maryland. She began work at the age of 7. Over the years she gained a lot of physical and mental strength which aided her in her later years. At 13, she was accidently beat when another slave was supposed to get it, which led her to sometimes fall asleep suddenly and experience hallucinations. She married the free African American John Tubman in 1844. In 1849, Tubman escaped the plantation she was enslaved in through the Underground Railroad, but John decided not to come, so Harriet came back for him, but he had remarried after her departure.

Over the years she gained a lot of physical and mental strength which aided her in her later years. She married the free African American John Tubman in 1844. In 1849, Tubman escaped the plantation she was enslaved in through the Underground Railroad, but John decided not to come, so Harriet came back for him, but he had remarried after her departure.

Shortly after her escape from slavery. She began to bring her family out of slavery. In 1850, her sister and 2 children. Later, her brother along with 2 other slaves. Her most dangerous mission was to rescue her parents in 1857, because they were old. It is known that her favorite way to sneak slaves to freedom was using disguise, for example, when black men are reporting to escape, the slaves will dress as white women. The biggest trouble for her was that freed slaves had to go to Canada, a much longer journey than just going to Pennsylvania. She conducted plenty of escapes in the 1850s and freed over 70 slaves. Deeply religious, Tubman thought all her actions were guided by God. (McGuire and Wheeler) She most likely thought that she was meant to save these people from slavery and she followed this diligently. People were offered 40,000-dollar rewards for her capture by Maryland slave owners. During the civil war, Tubman was a spy and nurse for the Union army, because of all her slave rescue missions, she became familiar with the south and the layout of the land, which was a huge asset for the United States. She also helped and tended to slaves who found refuge within the Union Army. After the war she went to live on the farm her parents resided on in Auburn, New York. She married Nelson Davis in 1869. She cared for African American orphans and also former slaves. She established a school for freedmen and also established a church, as well as advocating for women’s rights. She also donated 25 acres of land to homeless African Americans in her final few years. She finally died due to a bout of pneumonia on March 10, 1913.
